Concept 01

Headline Accuracy

Headlines are written to grab attention fast — sometimes faster than the facts can keep up. A headline can be technically true and still leave you with a completely wrong impression.

Why it matters

Most shares happen after people read only the headline, never the article. A misleading headline can shape what thousands of people believe, even if the article underneath is careful and accurate.

In everyday life

Next time a headline makes you feel instantly shocked or certain, pause. Ask yourself: does the article actually support this exact claim, or just something vaguely similar?

Concept 02

Emotional Manipulation

Content that makes you feel something strongly — fear, outrage, urgency, guilt — is far more likely to be clicked, shared, and remembered. That's often a deliberate design choice, not an accident.

Why it matters

When we feel strong emotion, we think less critically and act faster — sharing or believing before checking anything. Recognizing the technique helps you separate "this feels true" from "this is true."

In everyday life

When a post makes your heart race, that's actually the moment to slow down, not speed up. Take a breath before you like, share, donate, or buy.

    Three habits for stronger media literacy

    • Read past the headline — compare its exact claim to what the article actually says before you react or share.
    • Notice your emotions. If something makes you feel rushed, afraid, or outraged, that's your cue to pause, not to act.
    • Check the specifics. Vague claims like "experts say" or "everyone knows" are weaker than named sources, real data, and clear context.
